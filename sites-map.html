The Cultural Landscape of Tidbinbilla

The Tidbinbilla landscape contains multiple sites of deep cultural importance to the Ngunnawal People. These locations represent long-term occupation, practical land use, and strong ties to the natural environment. Each site below is marked on the interactive map.

Click a marker on the map to view detailed cultural information about each site

Summary of Marked Sites

Birrigai Rock Shelter

One of the oldest known Aboriginal rock shelters in the world, with evidence of occupation dating back over 21,000 years to the last Ice Age.

Gibraltar Peak / Range Entry

A granite peak overlooking the Tidbinbilla valley, with views across the surrounding ranges. The area includes rock shelters that hold cultural significance for the Ngunnawal people.

Tidbinbilla Valley Floor & River Network

The valley floor and river network provided water, fish, and plant resources, and was likely used as a shared camping area. Grinding grooves can be seen on rocks near the river.

Bogong Moth High Alpine Refuge

High mountain rock shelters where Bogong Moths gather in huge numbers each summer, providing a seasonal food source for Aboriginal groups across the region.

Archaeological & Cultural Context

The sites marked on this map have had extensive research completed via:

  • Archaeological Excavation: Over time studies of stone tools, faunal remains, and occupation layers
  • Ethnographic Documentation: Historical records and Ngunnawal oral histories
  • Landscape Analysis: Geographic features, water sources, and resource distribution patterns
  • Material Culture Studies: Analysis of stone tools, grinding implements, and other artifacts

We acknowledge the Ngunnawal People as the traditional custodians of this land.

The Bogong Moth & Summer Gathering

In summer, many people came to the high mountain shelters while the moths were there. That season became one of the busiest times of year, with visitors staying, sharing food and taking part in shared activities.

Traditional food processing

Groups from across the region would travel to Tidbinbilla to collect moths and prepare them as food. The moths were often processed into a paste and shared among the people who were staying in the area.

Active Land & Ecological Management

The Ngunnawal seasonal system represents sophisticated ecological knowledge. Rather than viewing the landscape as static, the Ngunnawal People actively managed the environment to enhance productivity and maintain ecological balance:

  • Fire Management: Controlled burns in winter and autumn cleared dead vegetation, prevented catastrophic wildfires, and promoted regeneration of useful plant species
  • Water Management: Simple water systems, including fish traps, helped people use rivers and streams carefully.
  • Plant Care: People gathered useful plants and looked after the places where those plants grew.
  • Hunting Strategies: Hunting and gathering moved with the seasons so food was taken at the best times.
  • Moving Around: Seasonal movement between low and high places helped people stay safe and use the right resources at the right time.

We acknowledge the Ngunnawal People as the traditional custodians of this land.
